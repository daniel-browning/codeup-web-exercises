How many people visit national parks?
In 2017, 330,882,751 visited national parks.

Which national park is the busiest park?
In 2017, Great Smokey Mountains National Park had 11,388,893 visitors.

How much does it cost to run these parks?
In 2017, the annual fiscal budget was $2.932 billion.

Can I camp out in the park?
Many national parks offer campsite availabilities.

Can I make reservations to camp in the park?
For camp reservations, please visit visitRecreation.gov to see a list of parks that partake in the reservations program. Please note, not all parks take reservations.

Can I bring my pet with me to the park?
Some parks do allow leashed pets on the developed grounds such as trails and campsites. Abandoning of pets is strictly prohibited.

Where can I find more information on the pet policy for national parks?
Please visit NPS Pet Regulations to learn more about pets and parks.

How many people does the National Park Service employ?
The National Park Service employs over 20,000 permanent, temporary, and seasonal employees

Can I volunteer at a national park?
Yes, please visit Working with National Parks to learn more about student and volunteer opportunities.

Is it free to visit a national park?
Fewer than one third (1/3) of national parks charge an entrance fee.

Where can I learn more about a specific national park?
For more information about one of our specific parks, please visit us at our park search to search our park database.

Lake Meredith

  • Located in Texas;
  • Inhabited by Red Foxes and Cottontail Rabbits;
  • No electricity available for camping; and
  • Park is free to enter.

Crater Lake

  • Located in Oregon;
  • Average annual snowfall is approximately 43 feet;
  • The lake is 1,943 feet deep; and
  • The hole was not made by a meteor, it is actually the result of a volcanic caldera.

Padre Island

  • Located in Texas;
  • Free to enter but no camping reservations are taken and there is a camping permit;
  • Pet friendly but must be on a leash that is less than six feet; and
  • No lifeguard on duty. If pulled by a current, swim parallel to the current then swim back to shore after you are free from the flow of the current.
